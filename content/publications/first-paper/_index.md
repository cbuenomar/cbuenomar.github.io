## Overview

This paper investigates how the spatial organisation of schooling in Madrid
reflects and reinforces social inequalities in the urban landscape. Using a
combination of GIS mapping, administrative data, and qualitative fieldwork,
we trace how school choice policies interact with housing markets and
residential segregation to produce fragmented educational geographies.

## Key Findings

- Educational supply in Madrid is unevenly distributed across socio-economic
  zones, with high-performing schools concentrated in affluent neighbourhoods.
- School choice policies amplify rather than mitigate spatial inequalities,
  as families with more resources are better positioned to navigate the system.
- Fragmented educational spaces correspond closely to patterns of urban
  gentrification and displacement.
